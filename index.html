IFSC World Cups 2015-2021

Competitive Sport Climbing

The IFSC Climbing World Cup is a series of climbing competitions held annually and organized by the International Federation of Sport Climbing (IFSC). The athletes compete in three disciplines: Lead, Boulder and Speed. The number of competitions and locations vary from year to year.

The following visualisation presents data from the official IFSC website, detailing the location of competitions, overview of competitors and rankings of the althetes and their country's team.

Locations

The map visualisation displays the location for each World Cup held of the discipline. The colour of the marker shows the continent and the size of the marker depicts the number of times it has held a World Cup.

Most of the World Cups competitions were held in Europe and Asia from 2015-2021. The continent of Europe has the highest number of countries hosting competitions.

China has held the most World Cups in total, in its various provinces: Nanjing, Chongqing, Wujiang, Xiamen, Tai'an and Haiyang.

Briancon, France (FRA) has hosted the most World Cups (7) for a single competition location, hosting a Cup each year between 2015 and 2021 for the Lead discipline.

In North America, Vail (USA) is a popular destination for competition location for the Boulder World Cup, hosting 5 competitions, on par with Meiringen (SUI, Switzerland), Munich (GER, Germany) and Chongqing (CHN, China).

Competitors

The pie chart shows the gender breakdown of the discipline. The gender split between Female and Male competitors is mostly even, for all the disciplines.

The area chart on the right depicts the total number of athletes competing over the years from each continent. Each colour represents a different continent.

The sport of climbing has gained popularity over the past few years, with many new indoor climbing facilities popping up everywhere. However at the elite level, only approximately 150 atheletes compete. The total number of competitors spiked in 2016 (Lead & Speed) and 2018 (Boulder), due to a sudden increase of athletes from Europe and Asia. The total number of athletes involved in competitions halted in 2020 during the Covid-19 pandemic, and decreased or stagnated in 2021, due to athletes not being able to travel overseas to participate in competitions.

Most of the athletes originate from Europe and Asia, and this is related to the locations of the competitions, where Europe and Asia hold most of the World Cups.

Let's find out more about the ranking and performance of the athletes and their national teams...

Rankings

At the end of each World Cup competition, a trophy is awarded to the winner, the top three athletes are awarded Gold, Silver and Bronze medals, and the top six athletes are awarded prize money. The top 30 competitors of individual World Cup competitions are eligible to accrue points.

For each discipline (Lead, Boulder and Speed), the points awarded to each athlete are added together throughout the World Cup series in order to determine an overall World Cup ranking.

At the end of each competition, a national team ranking is determined, for each discipline, by adding the ranking points of the three highest ranked individual team members.

This bar chart shows the rankings top 20 athletes of the discipline based on their accumulated points.

Females dominate the sport, with Janja Garnbret (Lead), Akiyo Noguchi (Boulder) and Anouck Jaubert (Speed) leading the Athletes Rankings, gaining the most points in competition.

Males are more evenly matched amongst one another, where point range between the top 1-4 is very small.

More information about each athlete can be found on the IFSC website.

The line chart on the left depicts the number of medals earned by a national team (Country). The colour of the line represents the continent of the team - slight variations in saturation is used to differentiate between countries. The stacked bar chart on the left displays the total number of medals the national team has won between 2015-2021, broken down into Gold, Silver and Bronze medal tallies.

The medal total and tally have a very interesting distribution. For the case of Boulder and Speed disciplines, only one national team dominates the medal tally, and the remaining national teams have a few medals each. For the Lead discipline, the medals are almost evenly spread between the teams.

The national teams with the most medals for each discipline are: Slovenia (SLO) (Lead), Japan (JPN) (Boulder) and Russia (RUS) (Speed). For Slovenia and Japan, most of their medals consist of Gold and Silver. This is no surprise as Janja Garnbret (top Lead athlete) is from Slovenia and Akiyo Noguchi (top Boulder athlete) is from Japan. However for Russia most of their medals are from Bronze places.

Note: No data is recorded for 2020 as competitions were placed on hold during the Covid-19 pandemic.
